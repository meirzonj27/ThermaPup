What is K9HeatGuard?

K9HeatGuard is a smart collar attachment designed to continuously monitor a dog’s body temperature and vital signs. When readings leave the safe range, the system sends instant text alerts to the owner.

Our goal is to reduce the risk of heat stroke, dehydration, and other temperature-related health emergencies in dogs—especially during hot weather, car rides, or intense exercise.

Real-time monitoring

Sensors track temperature and heart rate every few seconds, creating a live picture of your dog’s health.

Text message alerts

If your dog approaches unsafe temperature ranges, K9HeatGuard sends a clear, simple text so you can act fast.

Comfortable & lightweight

Designed to clip onto a normal collar with soft materials that don’t bother the dog while playing or resting.

Data-informed care

Logs of temperature and activity help owners understand how weather, exercise, and environment affect their dog.

How K9HeatGuard Works

1

Wearable sensor module

A small module on the collar measures temperature and heart rate using contact sensors placed against the dog’s fur.

2

On-board microcontroller

A microcontroller reads the sensor data, filters out noise, and checks it against safe ranges based on veterinarian guidelines.

3

Wireless connection

The device sends readings to a connected hub or phone, which prepares alerts if anything looks risky.

4

SMS alerts to owner

If temperature leaves the safe zone, the system sends a text message including the dog’s status and recommended actions.

Example Alert Message

“Alert from K9HeatGuard: Luna’s body temperature is 104.1°F (high). Move her to shade, offer cool (not ice-cold) water, and contact a vet if she doesn’t cool down within a few minutes.”

Our STEM Design Process

K9HeatGuard started as a STEM class project. Our challenge was to use engineering and technology to solve a real-world problem. Here’s how we applied the engineering design process.

1. Identify the problem

We researched how often dogs suffer from heat stroke and learned that many cases happen because owners don’t realize their dog is overheating until it’s too late.

We defined our problem as: “How can we give dog owners an early warning before their pet’s temperature becomes dangerous?”

2. Research & brainstorm

We studied basic canine biology, safe temperature ranges, and existing pet wearables. We brainstormed ideas like cooling vests, smart bowls, and sensor collars.

We chose a collar-mounted sensor because it would work in many environments (indoors, outdoors, in the car, or at the park).

3. Plan the solution

We sketched diagrams showing how sensors, the microcontroller, power supply, and wireless connection would fit together.

We also planned what information the text alerts should include so they would be clear and helpful during a stressful moment.

4. Build prototypes

Using classroom materials, we created a non-functioning physical model of the collar attachment and a mocked-up phone screen showing sample SMS alerts and data graphs.

If we were building a working prototype, we would use temperature sensors, a microcontroller board, a battery, and an SMS or Bluetooth module.

5. Test & improve

We tested our design by walking through real-life scenarios: hot days, car rides, and high-energy play. We asked:

  • Would the owner get the alert early enough?
  • Are the instructions in the message easy to follow?
  • Is the collar comfortable and secure for the dog?

Based on feedback, we improved the wording of alerts and adjusted where the sensor sits on the collar.

6. Reflect & next steps

We reflected on what went well—such as identifying a real need—and what we would improve with more time, like testing with more dog sizes and fur types.

In a future version, we’d like to add a mobile app, GPS tracking, and a vet dashboard for professional monitoring.

The Science Behind Heat Safety

Dogs can’t cool themselves as efficiently as humans. They mostly rely on panting and a small amount of sweating through their paw pads. Because of this, their body temperature can rise quickly in hot environments.

Veterinarians say that a normal dog temperature is usually around 101–102.5°F. Temperatures above this range, especially over 104°F, can be dangerous and may lead to heat exhaustion or heat stroke.

What K9HeatGuard Watches For

  • Rising core temperature beyond the normal safe range.
  • Fast heart rate that could signal stress or overheating.
  • Patterns over time, such as how quickly a dog heats up on a walk.
  • Sudden spikes that might mean the dog is in trouble.

K9HeatGuard is not meant to replace a veterinarian—it is designed to help owners notice problems sooner.
